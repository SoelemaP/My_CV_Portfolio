HR & Driver Performance Dashboard

Part of the Logistics Operations Analytics Suite. This report addresses the people side of logistics operations: Are our drivers performing efficiently? Where are the safety risks, and are they preventable? It covers on-time delivery rates, revenue per driver, and incident analysis. It is built on top of the HR data mart in the LogisticsWarehouse SQL Server database.

The report covers January through October 2024 as the reporting period, reflecting a realistic year-to-date executive view.

Key Findings

  • On-time performance is a company-wide issue, not a driver issue

    The average on-time rate is 44.71%, with even the top performers not exceeding 51%. The consistency of low rates across all 124 drivers points to systemic causes such as route planning or traffic conditions rather than individual driver behaviour.

  • Driver experience does not predict performance or safety

    Cross-metric analysis shows no correlation between years of experience and on-time delivery rate, nor between experience and incident frequency. With an average of 13.5 years of experience across the workforce, this suggests that additional experience alone does not improve performance. This points toward route dynamics and system-level factors as the dominant drivers of both metrics.

  • Preventable incidents represent a significant and addressable cost

    Out of all incidents, 40.82% (20) were preventable, accounting for $263K in claims. Of those, only 4 incidents were also at fault, generating $72K in claims. This distinction is important: most preventable incidents occurred without driver fault, suggesting procedural or environmental factors rather than driver negligence.

  • Equipment damage is the highest-cost incident

    Despite not being the most frequent incident, equipment damage is the highest-cost incident with $226K in claims. Equipment damage occurs less often than accidents but generates higher total claims, meaning each event carries a significantly higher financial cost.

  • Higher-revenue drivers carry slightly higher claim amounts

    The revenue vs claim amount scatter plot shows a weak upward trend: drivers generating more revenue also tend to have higher claim amounts. This is likely explained by higher mileage: drivers completing more trips are exposed to more risk. It is not a risk management concern, but it should be monitored as revenue grows.

Recommendations

  • Investigate systemic causes of low on-time delivery rates.

    Since all drivers perform similarly, the root cause is unlikely to be driver-related. A focused analysis of facility detention times, route scheduling, and traffic patterns (particularly during the low-performing months March and September) would identify where the breakdown occurs.

  • Redesign driver training programmes around real-world route and facility challenges, not experience.

    Since experience does not predict performance, training investment should focus on route-specific conditions and facility procedures rather than generic experience-building programmes. New and experienced drivers underperform equally, meaning the environment matters more than experience.

  • Implement a preventable incident reduction programme.

    Since 16 out of 20 preventable incidents occurred without driver fault, the focus should be on operational procedures and environmental conditions rather than driver discipline. A review of equipment inspection and facility conditions at the highest-incident locations would be the logical starting point.

    While $72K represents a relatively small share of total claims, at-fault preventable incidents carry reputational and legal risks beyond the financial cost. The 4 cases warrant individual review to determine whether additional driver training or procedural changes are needed.

  • Prioritise equipment inspection and maintenance for damage prevention.

    Equipment damage generates the highest claim costs per incident. A structured pre-trip and post-trip inspection protocol, combined with more frequent equipment servicing, would directly target the costliest incident category.

  • Begin succession planning for the driver workforce.

    With an average age of 45 and average tenure of 7.4 years, a meaningful share of the workforce will reach retirement age within the next decade. Since experience does not predict performance, the company cannot rely on institutional knowledge transfer alone. A structured recruitment and onboarding pipeline built around training for real operational scenarios is the priority.
